When I was young, my dad always said, “Finish high school, graduate college, get a job, get married, and have kids. I do not care when they happen, but they need to be in that order.” I was fortunate to grow up with 2 happily married parents who both had high school diplomas, college degrees, and launched successful careers. My dad’s words made sense, so I set off to follow my parents’ footsteps.

I finished high school and went on to get degrees in Economics and Applied Mathematics Engineering at Northwestern. As an athlete in college, I spent the summers training to keep my scholarship and compete in national competitions. While this experience taught me valuable lessons in teamwork and leadership, it limited my internship opportunities. Out of college, I did not know what I wanted to do, but I got a job as an Economic Analyst at Abbott. While there I worked with a now close friend named Will Dearden. Will holds a Masters degree from Northwestern in Managerial Economics and Strategy and Undergraduate degrees in Math and Econ from Lehigh. Will and I only overlapped at Abbott for about 3 months, but he introduced me to the fields of Data Science, Machine Learning, and Artificial Intelligence. These fields perfectly combined my passion of automation, mathematics, and economic, so I finally knew how I wanted to launch my career.

The Ideal Data Scientist

The term “Data Scientist” has seen an exponential growth in popularity in recent years, but what does a Data Scientist mean? And what are the qualifications of the “Ideal Data Scientist”?

Abbott is a great company that takes care of their employees, but I knew the role I was playing was not what I needed for my career to grow, so I started to look for a new job as a Data Scientist. I took online courses to learn R, Python, machine learning, and deep learning. I started attending data science meetups in Chicago to listen to researchers present their latest work and to network with other data scientists. I enjoyed having in-depth conversations about modeling complex AI problems, resolving data issues, and managing the tradeoffs with code deployment. Unfortunately, whenever someone said I should apply to be a data scientist at their company, they asked where I got my PhD.

At this point, I hadn’t even thought about applying for a Master’s Degree. I was only a year out from undergrad, and I knew Northwestern was only starting to adapt their classes to use data science tools, so I didn’t understand why anyone would need a PhD for such a young field. While I felt like the online modules I was taking with DataCamp (before their CEO had his sexual assault scandal) and Coursera were teaching me very relevant theory and job-ready application skills, getting data science interviews with only undergraduate degrees was difficult, so I applied to Georgia Tech’s Online Master’s of Computer Science (OMSCS).

In 2019, I was accepted to OMSCS around the same time I took a job to consult for Accenture as a Data Scientist. This was thrilling as I knew these were huge steps towards my career goals. As I write this article, I am on my 3rd data science project at Accenture and in my 3rd OMSCS class. It clear to me now that “data science” covers many different areas, and many companies know they need AI but don’t know what qualifications they need to look for, so they look for general qualifications in the area. This often means trusting the establish college-centric education system and downplaying the role of outside learning.

College Rigor vs Online Nanodegrees

One of my favorite classes in undergrad was Mechanical Engineering 333: Mechatronics. I loved the class partially because it was my first real introduction to robotics, but also because it had a “flipped” class structure, meaning lectures and quizzes were electronic and done at home, while class time with the professor and TAs was reserved for coding, wiring, and debugging. Recorded lectures allowed everyone to rewatch unclear sections, and the extra face time with the teaching staff helped resolve project struggles much quicker.

Nanodegree programs offer many of the same lecture benefits that flipped classes do, but often do not share the same level of assessment rigor that traditional classes do.
